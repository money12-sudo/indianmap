Sources

Bibliography, evidence table, open conflicts, and what has not yet been consulted

Every record on the map is listed below with the sources behind it and what remains outstanding. Where published accounts disagree, the disagreement is stated rather than settled. Records marked Lead are unverified.

Principal sources

Secondary — interview-based
LaRotta, Alex. “Pass the Naan: The Story of Houston’s Mahatma Gandhi District.” Houston History 13, no. 1: 33–35. PDF
Based on interviews with the corridor's founding merchants (Sharan Gahunia of Raja Sweets, Kaiser Lashkari of Himalaya, Animesh Patel of Subhlaxmi Grocers), with Mayor Annise Parker, and with India Culture Center trustees. Produced through the University of Houston's Gulf Coast Food Project. Supplies the dates used here for Karat 22, Raja Sweets, India Grocers, and Himalaya, several of which differ from the dates in general circulation online.
Primary — oral history collection
Houston Asian American Archive, Chao Center for Asian Studies, Rice University. haaa.rice.edu. Physical collection: MS 573, Woodson Research Center, Fondren Library.
Over 500 published oral histories, roughly fifty with Indian American narrators, transcripts online. Not yet worked for this project. Rice's own metadata notes that some transcripts carry AI-generated accessibility enhancements and may contain errors, so quotations must be checked against the recording.
Primary — institutional self-report
India Culture Center, official history. Sikh Center of the Gulf Coast Area, official site. Sikh National Center, about. BAPS Houston. Sri Meenakshi Temple Society.
Self-reported. Useful for dates of incorporation, founding narratives, and stated reasons for relocation, none of it independently verified. The Sikh National Center's account of why it left its first gurdwara (an outgrown building, and a landfill developed behind the property) is a causal claim that county permit records could corroborate.
Secondary — press
Houston Chronicle; Texas Highways; Houstonia; Eater Houston; Houston Food Finder; GQ.
Used for openings, closures, and relocations after 2000, where the press is the only public record. Food journalism is unreliable on founding years, and routinely conflates a proprietor's first restaurant with their best-known one. The Himalaya conflict below is an instance.
Reference — used with caution
Wikipedia, Mahatma Gandhi District, Houston and related entries.
Used to locate underlying citations, not as authority. It is the origin of the 1985 date for Raja Sweets that the LaRotta interview contradicts.

Open source conflicts

Two dates in this dataset are contested in print. They are recorded unresolved.

Sikh Center of the Gulf Coast Area

Date correction. November 1973 refers to the building's inauguration; the congregation dates its golden jubilee from that month. The organization itself was founded in 1972. Both dates are recorded here rather than collapsed into one.

Raja Sweets

Source conflict, resolved provisionally. Wikipedia gives 1985; Alex LaRotta, who interviewed the founder's daughter for Houston History, gives 1986. The interview is the stronger source. 1985 should be treated as an artifact unless a contemporary advertisement or filing establishes it.

Bombay Sweets

The 1997 date is well attested. The claim that this was Houston's first pure vegetarian Indian restaurant originates in the business's own marketing and is repeated verbatim by review aggregators — the characteristic signature of a self-report in circulation. It is recorded here as a claim, not a finding.

Indika

Two source conflicts. (1) The original 2001–2006 location is variously described as a small space in southwest Houston and as Memorial; it cannot be placed with confidence, and this marker therefore stands at the Montrose address the restaurant occupied from 2006. (2) In a 2013 interview Jaisinghani gave the opening year as 2002; her own restaurant's published biography gives July 2001. A narrator's memory of her own founding year is off by a year — a caution worth carrying into any use of oral testimony.

Himalaya Restaurant

Source conflict, unresolved. GQ gives 1994. LaRotta, who interviewed Lashkari directly for Houston History, records that Himalaya opened in 2004 and that Lashkari operated a different restaurant nearby in the early 1990s. The published dates appear to conflate the two establishments. A health permit or assumed-name filing would settle it.

Not yet consulted

These are the archives that would settle most of what is still open. Almost none of what they hold is online.

  1. Harris County and Fort Bend County assumed-name (DBA) filings. Dated, indexed, public. The most direct record of when a business legally began trading, and the way to settle the Jay Stores, India Grocers, and Subhlaxmi dates.
  2. Houston city directories and telephone books, 1978–1995. Houston Public Library, Texas Room. A business appearing and then disappearing across successive annual volumes is the closest available proxy for opening and closing, and the only way to find the closures this dataset cannot currently see.
  3. Houston Chronicle historical archive. Accessible remotely with a Houston Public Library card. Opening announcements, closures, and — more valuable — the advertisements themselves, which carry addresses and dates.
  4. Community newspapers. Indo-American News and Voice of Asia. Advertisements, opening notices, and community coverage that appears nowhere else.
  5. Harris County property and deed records, and City of Houston permit files. Including the landfill siting behind the Prairie Drive gurdwara.
  6. HAAA oral history transcripts. Roughly fifty Indian American interviews. Narrators include Sonal and Vinod Bhuchar (Fort Bend), Anita Jaisinghani (restaurants), Sesh and Prabha Bala, Sulekh Jain, Rathna Kumar, and Sam Kannappan.
  7. Family-held papers. Photographs, menus, invoices, and ephemera held by the founding families. Uncatalogued, and the likeliest place for the corridor's earliest documentation to survive.
