Coverage, scholarship, institutional records, and what is still missing

What has been written about this community, and by whom.

Press and criticism

Note the geography. Almost every restaurant that draws national attention sits east of Hillcroft, in Montrose, on Kirby, or inside the Galleria. Almost none of the coverage is of the corridor itself.

Press
Why Houston Is the Best City in America for Indian Food
Houstonia · 2024
A survey of the city's Indian dining across all three of the geographies this project identifies — the corridor, the suburbs, and the inner loop — without naming the split. Useful for establishing which restaurants a general readership was being pointed to in 2024, and where they were.
Press
Musaafer is one of six Houston-area restaurants with a Michelin star
Houston Public Media · Nov 2024
The first Michelin guide to Texas. An Indian restaurant inside the Galleria takes a star. Contains an interview with chef Mayank Istwal on the hundred-day journey through India that produced the menu.
Guide
Musaafer
MICHELIN Guide · 2024
The Guide's own entry, which opens by remarking that dinner in a shopping mall does not sound promising. The location is the point.
Press
Musaafer opens a second location in New York City
CultureMap Houston · Aug 2025
The restaurant expanded to TriBeCa rather than to Katy or Sugar Land. Where a business chooses to grow is evidence about who it thinks its customer is.
Press
The Mahatma Gandhi District Is the Epicenter of Indian Culture in Texas
Texas Highways · 2022
Contains the interview in which a former India Culture Center president describes Indian Houstonians as living in Sugar Land, Pearland, and Clear Lake and returning to Hillcroft on weekends — the residential dispersal and commercial concentration stated plainly, by someone who lived it.
Press
Houston's Mahatma Gandhi District Celebrates South Asian Culture
Authentic Texas · 2025
Census figures for the Indian and Pakistani populations of Harris and Fort Bend counties, and an estimate of 150,000 Indian-origin residents in greater Houston from India House's executive director.
Press
Bombay Sweets
Houstonia · 2025
Independent confirmation that the restaurant has operated on Hillcroft since 1997 — eleven years after Raja Sweets, during the period the corridor is supposed to have been emptying.
Press
Katy Asian Town
Eater Houston · 2018–
The suburban district as covered by the food press: developer-built, master-planned, pan-Asian. Read against any account of how Hillcroft came together and the contrast does the analytical work by itself.

Scholarship and archives

Scholarship
LaRotta, Alex. “Pass the Naan: The Story of Houston's Mahatma Gandhi District.”
Houston History 13:1 · 2015
The core secondary source for this project. Interviews with Sharan Gahunia (Raja Sweets), Kaiser Lashkari (Himalaya), Animesh Patel (Subhlaxmi), India Culture Center trustees, and Mayor Annise Parker. Includes photographs held by Raja Sweets and the India Culture Center — among them Yogi Gahunia with Mayor Lee Brown, and the Gandhi District street toppers. These photographs are the earliest images located so far and are worth requesting from the magazine or the families directly.
Archive
Houston Asian American Archive
Rice University · 2009–
Over 500 oral histories, roughly fifty with Indian American narrators, transcripts and video online. Also holds photographs, memorabilia, and ephemera donated by narrators, catalogued at the Woodson Research Center as MS 573. The physical collection is the likeliest source of artifacts for this project and requires an appointment.
Archive
HAAA oral history images and ephemera
Rice University Digital Collections · —
Photographs and news clippings deposited alongside the interviews. Rights are held by the donors; Rice grants non-profit educational use but publication requires permission from the rights holders. Any image reproduced here must be cleared.

Institutional records

Self-published histories. Unverified, and indispensable: they carry the founding narratives, the stated reasons for relocation, and details recorded nowhere else.

Institutional
A Brief History of The Sikh Center, Houston
SikhNet · 2023
The congregation's fiftieth-anniversary account. Two acres on Prairie Drive for $10,000, volunteers roping lawnmowers together to clear the ground, an electrical fire in December 1987. Written for a community readership rather than a scholarly one, and rich in detail no other source carries.
Institutional
Sikh National Center — About
Sikh National Center · —
The account of why the congregation left Prairie Drive: an outgrown building, and a landfill being developed on the property behind it. The most consequential causal claim in the dataset, and it is here in the institution's own words.
Institutional
History
India Culture Center · —
Incorporation on 27 June 1973; Harb S. Hayre as first president; the community numbering roughly five thousand. The founding document of Indian civic life in Houston, self-published.
Institutional
Consulate General of India, Houston
Government of India · —
Consular jurisdiction across five states. The site carries press releases going back years, which document which community organizations the Indian state chose to recognize and when.

Wanted

The artifacts this project needs and does not have. None of these are online. If you hold any of them, please get in touch.

  • Photographs of Hillcroft before 2010. Storefronts, interiors, opening days, festivals in the parking lots. Almost none are published. They exist in family albums and in shop back offices.
  • Menus and advertisements, 1984–2000. An advertisement is a dated document that carries an address. For the businesses whose opening years are still contested — Raja Sweets, India Grocers, Himalaya, Jay Stores — a single dated ad settles the question.
  • Community newspapers. Indo-American News and Voice of Asia, which carried the opening notices, the wedding announcements, and the advertising that the mainstream press never ran.
  • Newsletters and event programs. India Culture Center, temple societies, cultural associations. Programs carry dates, addresses, sponsor lists, and the names of everyone who mattered in a given year.
  • The Gandhi District street toppers. Installed after January 2010. Photographs of their installation, and of the street before them, would document the moment the district acquired a name.
On reproduction. No photograph or document appears on this site without written permission from the rights holder. Rice University grants non-profit educational access to the HAAA collection, but access is not permission to publish; that must come from the donors. Where an image is used, its creator, date, and terms of use are recorded. Nothing is taken from the open web and republished.
