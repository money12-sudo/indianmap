Scholarship

The literature this project sits inside

The pattern on this map already has a name. A community disperses residentially, keeps a concentrated commercial core, and grows a second cluster in the suburbs that coexists with the first rather than replacing it. Wei Li called it an ethnoburb in 1997, and there is now a substantial literature on it.

That is not bad news for this project. It means the finding is real, and it means the argument can be made against an established model rather than from scratch.

The ethnoburb

The concept
Li, Wei. “Anatomy of a New Ethnic Settlement: The Chinese Ethnoburb in Los Angeles.” Urban Studies 35, no. 3 (1998): 479–501.
The paper that named the thing. Li proposes the ethnoburb: a suburban ethnic cluster of residential areas and business districts, multiethnic, in which one group is concentrated without forming a majority. Ethnoburbs are not the enclave and not the assimilated suburb. They are a third form, and they coexist with rather than replace the old downtown district. Everything this project has found in Houston, Li found in the San Gabriel Valley thirty years ago.
The book
Li, Wei. Ethnoburb: The New Ethnic Community in Urban America. Honolulu: University of Hawai‘i Press, 2009.
The full treatment. Interviews, census analysis, fieldwork. The argument that matters here: the growth of ethnoburbs since the 1960s contradicts the assumption that ethnicity dissolves as immigrants move out of the inner city. What the San Gabriel Valley shows is ethnic persistence, not assimilation. Available to borrow at the Internet Archive and via Project MUSE.
The comparison
Li, Wei. “Ethnoburb versus Chinatown: Two Types of Urban Ethnic Communities in Los Angeles.”
The two-center model, stated directly: an old core and a new suburban cluster, operating at the same time as different kinds of place. This is the Hillcroft–Katy relationship, described in Los Angeles before this project began. (Linked to the overview article; find the paper itself through a library.)

Houston: demography and urban form

Data
Kinder Institute for Urban Research, Asian American Community Study. Rice University, 2026.
Published 12 January 2026. A multi-year, multilingual survey of 2,100 Asian residents across Harris, Fort Bend, Montgomery, and Brazoria counties, and it draws on the Houston Asian American Archive's oral histories. It contains the residential data this map does not have: census-tract maps of the Asian population in 1980 and in 2023. Everything on this site is commercial and institutional. Kinder has where people actually lived.
Data
Kinder Institute releases comprehensive study of one of Houston area's fastest-growing populations.
The headline figures. The region's Asian population rose from 1.8% to 9.9% over four decades. Between 2010 and 2023 it grew 117% in Fort Bend County. Indians are the largest Asian group at roughly 150,000. And the line worth quoting: more Asian residents live in Fort Bend County today than lived in the entire state of Texas in 1980.
Data
Kinder Houston Area Survey. Rice University, 1982–.
Forty-three consecutive years of survey data on Harris County, extended to Fort Bend and Montgomery. Over 50,000 respondents. No other American metropolitan area has anything like it, and it covers exactly the decades this project is about.
Context
Kinder Institute — Immigration research.
Includes work on Gulfton and Sharpstown, the neighborhoods the Hillcroft corridor sits inside, and on housing regulation that is, in their phrase, uniquely Houston. The no-zoning question runs through this material.

Where this project sits

The ethnoburb literature has already established the pattern: an old ethnic core persists while a suburban cluster forms alongside it, and ethnicity does not dissolve on contact with the suburbs. This project does not discover that. It tests whether the model holds for a community and a city it was not built from — Indian Houston rather than Chinese Los Angeles.

The variable Houston supplies, and Li's cases do not, is the absence of zoning. Los Angeles has a zoning code. Houston has none. If the ethnoburb model rests on land-use regulation pushing immigrant retail into particular places, then Houston is the case that tests it — because here the constraint is not the city's zoning map but the private deed restrictions of master-planned suburbs, which are a different instrument with different effects and a different history.

That is the opening a paper could occupy: not a new finding, but the same finding in the one large American city that lacks the mechanism the existing explanations rely on.

Further reading

Standard works in the field, cited from the literature rather than located in this session. Verify each citation before use. They are listed here as a reading path, not as evidence.

  • Portes, Alejandro, and Kenneth Wilson. The immigrant-enclave economy. The theoretical foundation for treating an ethnic business district as an economic form rather than a cultural one.
  • Rangaswamy, Padma. Namasté America: Indian Immigrants in an American Metropolis (Chicago). The closest existing analogue to what this project is attempting.
  • Khandelwal, Madhulika. Becoming American, Being Indian (Queens, New York). Indian community formation in a second American metropolis.
  • Kurien, Prema. On Hindu religious institution-building in the American diaspora — directly relevant to the temples and gurdwaras in this dataset.
  • Chakravorty, Sanjoy, Devesh Kapur, and Nirvikar Singh. The Other One Percent: Indians in America. The demographic and visa-category history that explains who arrived when.
  • Lung-Amam, Willow. Trespassers? Asian Americans and the Battle for Suburbia (Silicon Valley). School districts, design codes, and who suburbs are built for. The suburban-conflict half of the story this project has not touched.
  • Lin, Jan. On Houston and on ethnic place-making in American cities.
  • Klineberg, Stephen. Prophetic City: Houston on the Cusp of a Changed America. The demographic transformation of Houston by the founder of the Kinder Houston Area Survey.
